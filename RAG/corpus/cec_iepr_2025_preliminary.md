## 1. Forecast Framework

The 2025 IEPR is a full refresh (odd-year cycle). The forecast produces:

- Annual electricity demand by planning area (8 areas, 20 forecast zones)
- 8,760-hour electricity loads for a 1-in-2 weather year
- 1-in-5, 1-in-10, and 1-in-20 net electricity peak
- Load modifier scenarios for distributed generation, energy efficiency, building electrification, and transportation electrification

The forecast uses sector-level econometric and end-use models (residential end use + PCA calibration; commercial econometric; industrial by NAICS code; agriculture/cannabis/water pumping econometric) with additive load modifiers layered on top.

### Planning Areas

1. PG&E
2. SCE
3. SDG&E
4. NCNC (Northern California Non-CAISO)
5. LADWP
6. IID (Imperial Irrigation District)
7. Burbank/Glendale
8. Valley Electric Association (VEA)

## 2. Key Changes from 2024 IEPR Update

### Economic and Demographic Inputs

- Lower population projections (41.4 million by 2045 vs. 41.7 million in 2024 IEPR)
- Households growing faster than population (decrease in persons-per-household): 16.4 million by 2045
- Lower short-term economic growth (2024–2028), roughly comparable long-term growth
- Higher electricity rates and gas rates relative to 2024 IEPR

### New Forecast Elements

- **Known Loads**: First year CEC incorporated IOU distribution-level energization requests (from CPUC High DER proceeding). Joint CEC–CPUC data request covering PG&E, SCE, SDG&E as of May 2025. Includes residential developments, warehouses, retail, medical facilities, schools, industrial. Known loads are included only in the Local Reliability scenario (and alternative planning scenarios).
- **Updated data center methodology**: Expanded utility coverage now includes SVP, City of Palo Alto, City of Burbank, SDG&E (in addition to PG&E, SCE). Still uses 67% utilization assumption and application confidence levels (Agreement / Application / Inquiry tiers).
- **EV tax credit removal**: Baseline transportation demand updated to reflect removal of federal EV tax credit.
- **ITC removal for solar**: Self-generation forecast updated for removal of Investment Tax Credit.
- **Cannabis methodology improvement**: New data leading to lower Ag/Water Pumping forecast.
- **Committed Programs correction**: Fixed error causing efficiency savings to decay too quickly; result is slower load growth from program expiration.

## 3. Scenario Design

### 2025 IEPR Scenario Structure

| Component | Planning | Local Reliability |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| Econ/Demo | Mid | Mid |
| Data Centers | Mid | High |
| Known Loads | TBD / Alternative includes | Yes |
| BTM PV & Storage | Mid | Low |
| AAEE | Scenario 3 | Scenario 2 |
| AAFS | Scenario 2* | Scenario 3* |
| AATE | Scenario 2* | Scenario 3* |

*Proposed revision from 2024 IEPR. The 2024 IEPR Planning used AAFS 3 / AATE 3; the 2025 IEPR shifts to more conservative electrification assumptions (AAFS 2 / AATE 2 for Planning).

### Alternative Scenarios Presented

Staff presented multiple alternatives at the December 17 workshop:

- **Planning_noKnown_FS2_TE2**: Mid data centers, no known loads, AAEE 3, AAFS 2, AATE 2
- **Planning_wKnown_FS2_TE2**: Same as above but with known loads included
- **Local_wKnown_FS3_TE3**: High data centers, known loads, AAEE 2, AAFS 3, AATE 3

## 4. Headline Forecast Results

### Consumption and Sales (Cooper presentation, TN 268076)

- **Statewide Mid Case Baseline Sales in 2045**: ~353 TWh (nearly identical to 2024 IEPR)
- **Statewide High Case Baseline Sales in 2045**: ~394 TWh (31 TWh / 8.6% higher than 2024 IEPR)
- **Without self-generation**: Sales would be ~456 TWh in 2045 (16% higher)
- **Managed sales growth potential**: Up to ~61% increase over 20 years in the Local Reliability scenario with aggressive transportation electrification and known loads — EVs are the single largest driver

#### Consumption by Sector

- Commercial share grows from 36% (2025) to 44% (2045) of total consumption — driven by data centers
- Industrial consumption relatively flat, share declines from 15% to 11%
- Residential consumption higher than 2024 IEPR due to declining persons-per-household
- Ag/Cannabis/Water Pumping lower than 2024 IEPR due to improved cannabis methodology

#### Load Modifier Impacts (2045)

- **Planning net impacts**: 52.1 TWh (15% above Mid baseline) with AAFS 3/AATE 3; 37.7 TWh (11%) with AAFS 2/AATE 2
- **Local Reliability net impacts**: 67.2 TWh (17% above High baseline) with AAFS 4/AATE 4; 56.3 TWh (14%) with AAFS 3/AATE 3
- **Alternative Local Reliability is 60 TWh (15%) higher than Alternative Planning in 2045**

### Hourly and Peak Forecast (Fugate presentation, TN 267942)

#### CAISO Coincident Peak (1-in-2), Select Scenarios (MW)

| Year | Planning_noKnown_FS2_TE2 | Planning_wKnown_FS2_TE2 | Local_wKnown_FS3_TE3 |
|------|--------------------------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| 2025 | 46,487 | 46,487 | 46,487 |
| 2030 | 51,062 | 55,843 | 58,361 |
| 2035 | 57,370 | 61,898 | 67,672 |
| 2040 | 61,790 | 66,318 | 72,099 |
| 2045 | 65,763 | 70,291 | 75,073 |

- **Peak hour**: Remains September, hour 17–18 (late afternoon) across all scenarios
- **Peak hour shift**: Transition from hour 17 to hour 18 peak is delayed relative to 2024 IEPR; known loads further delay this transition
- **No winter peak**: No scenario produces a winter peak, though winter morning peaks grow due to building electrification
- **EV is the largest single peak driver through 2045** — even after shifting from AATE 3 to AATE 2, transportation electrification remains the biggest growth component

#### Key Changes from CED 2024

- Higher peak starting point for CAISO overall (EMS loads indicate increased weather-normal peak for PG&E)
- Corrected bias in climate data lowered SDG&E peak
- No change to base hourly consumption profiles
- Lower unadjusted consumption forecast due to economic conditions

## 5. Data Centers

### Pipeline (Cooper presentation, TN 268076; data center presentation TN 267165)

- **PG&E**: Largest pipeline. September 2025 data showed significant Agreement + Application + Inquiry capacity. December 2025 update removed ~1,500 MW of double-counted data centers from PG&E.
- **SCE**: Growing pipeline, both Agreement and Inquiry levels increasing from 2024 to 2025.
- **New entrants**: SDG&E, City of Burbank now included alongside SVP and City of Palo Alto.
- **VEA**: Osprey data center complex (new this cycle) drives significant peak growth in VEA service territory.

### Forecast Levels

- **Mid Case**: ~3,900 MW by 2040 (vs. ~3,500 MW in 2024 IEPR — 418 MW higher by 2040, but 276 MW lower in 2027 near-term due to updated energization timelines)
- **High Case**: ~5,500+ MW by 2040 (1,722 MW higher than 2024 IEPR High)

### Methodology

- Still uses confidence-level tiering: Agreement (highest), Application, Inquiry (lowest)
- 67% utilization assumption retained from 2024 IEPR
- Data centers treated as an additive load modifier on top of commercial sector baseline
- No behavioral/price-responsive demand modeling for data center loads

## 6. Known Loads (New for 2025 IEPR)

### Data Source

- Distribution-level energization requests submitted to CPUC under the High DER proceeding
- Joint CEC–CPUC data request to PG&E, SCE, SDG&E (as of May 2025)
- Includes: capacity, customer sector, energization date, load profiles

### Scale

- PG&E has the largest backlog (peaking above 3,000 MW cumulative by 2030)
- Majority of projects expected online by 2027
- Includes residential, non-residential, and transportation segments

### Methodology Revisions (December Update)

- **Cancellation rates**: Based on August 2025 Grid Needs Assessments filing with CPUC
- **Energization dates**: Updated to align with utility DPP modeling (PG&E, SCE, SDG&E each updated)
- **Ramp rate**: PG&E revised from 1-year to 3-year ramp; SCE/SDG&E TBD
- **Utilization factor**: PG&E calculated from AMI data for completed projects; SCE provided adjustment; SDG&E applies SCE adjustment
- **PG&E known load energy impacts dropped significantly** from November draft to December update after removing double-counted data centers and applying updated methodology

### Policy Significance

Known loads were included because the backlog of energization requests exceeds baseline growth in the IEPR forecast, creating reliability risks from the gap between transmission and distribution planning. However, 2025 is the first year CEC used this data, there is no historical track record, and the backlog represents a unique circumstance.

## 7. Climate and Weather Methodology

### What Changed

- Corrected bias in climate data lowered SDG&E peak
- EMS (Energy Management System) loads indicate increased weather-normal peak for PG&E

### What Did NOT Change

- **No overhaul of the climate methodology**
- The forecast continues to use WRF (Weather Research and Forecasting) model variants for weather normalization
- **Does not use CMIP6 climate projections** or ensemble-based climate scenarios
- **Does not incorporate humidity variables** (specific humidity, dew point depression)
- **Does not model trend-retained warming** — weather inputs are detrended
- Spatial resolution remains at the planning area / forecast zone level (8 planning areas, 20 zones), not county-level

### ClimateFEAT Relevance

This represents the same structural gap identified in the 2024 IEPR comparison. The 2025 IEPR climate methodology is structurally identical to 2024 for purposes of comparing against ClimateFEAT's approach:

- ClimateFEAT uses 24+ CMIP6 ensemble members (SSP370 and SSP245) with trend retained
- ClimateFEAT includes specific humidity (spfh) and dew point depression (dpd_k) with rolling aggregates
- ClimateFEAT operates at 58-county spatial resolution with population-weighted aggregation
- ClimateFEAT uses a multi-stream transformer with cross-attention rather than sector econometric + additive modifiers

All seven comparison axes documented in `climatefeat_vs_iepr_2024_comparison.md` remain valid for the 2025 IEPR.

## 8. Self-Generation (BTM PV and Storage)

- Self-generation growth in 2025 IEPR is slower than 2024 IEPR
- Reflects updated capacity factors (revised down 3–4 percentage points in 2024 IEPR, carried forward)
- ITC removal further reduces PV growth projections
- Mid self-gen and Low self-gen scenarios both lower than corresponding 2024 IEPR scenarios

---

## 9. Gas Forecast

- Baseline gas sales lower than 2023 IEPR due to lower recent historical data and lower economic growth
- Near-zero compound annual growth rate (0.01% from 2024–2045 in baseline)
- Residential gas flat near-term, slight increase long-term due to household growth
- Commercial gas declining due to rates and economic conditions
- Industrial manufacturing gas roughly flat
- Gas forecast is less relevant to ClimateFEAT scope (electricity-only model)

## 12. Key Deltas: 2025 IEPR vs. 2024 IEPR Update

| Dimension | 2024 IEPR Update | 2025 IEPR |
|-----------|------------------|-----------|
| Forecast horizon | To 2040 | To 2045 |
| Data center High Case 2040 | ~3,800 MW | ~5,500+ MW (1,722 MW higher) |
| Known loads | Not included | Included in Local Reliability + alternative scenarios |
| AATE/AAFS in Planning | Scenario 3/3 | Shifted to Scenario 2/2 |
| Self-gen growth | Higher | Lower (ITC removal, lower capacity factors) |
| CAISO 1-in-2 peak 2040 (Planning w/ Known Loads) | ~60,000 MW (est.) | 66,318 MW |
| Statewide managed sales 2045 | ~411,121 GWh (managed, Local Reliability) | Potentially higher with known loads; alternative Local Reliability significantly exceeds 2024 |
| Climate methodology | 4 WRF models, detrended | Unchanged — same structural approach |
| Biggest peak driver | Data centers + TE | EV adoption (AATE) dominant; data centers secondary |
